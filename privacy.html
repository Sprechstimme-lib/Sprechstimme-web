Privacy Policy

Last updated: December 15, 2025

This Privacy Policy describes how the Sprechstimme website ("we", "us", or "our") collects, uses, and handles information when you visit sprechstim.me (the "Website").

Information We Collect

We collect basic analytics data to understand how visitors interact with our Website. When you visit our Website, the following information is collected:

  • IP Address: Your IP address is retrieved via a third-party service (ipify) and sent to our analytics endpoint hosted on Cloudflare. We do not store your IP address; it is only used for aggregated analytics in the Cloudflare dashboard.
  • Page Views: Information about which pages you visit on our Website.
  • Timestamp: The date and time of your visit.

How We Use This Information

We use the analytics data to:

  • Understand how visitors use our Website
  • Improve our Website's content and user experience
  • Monitor the performance and availability of our Website

Data Storage

We do not store any personally identifiable information, including IP addresses, on our own servers. Analytics data is processed by Cloudflare and only aggregated, anonymized statistics are available to us through the Cloudflare dashboard.

Legal Basis for Processing (GDPR)

For visitors from the European Economic Area (EEA), United Kingdom, and Switzerland, we process this data under the legal basis of legitimate interests (Article 6(1)(f) of the GDPR). Our legitimate interests include:

  • Understanding website usage to improve our services
  • Ensuring the security and proper functioning of our Website
  • Analyzing trends to better serve our users

We have assessed that these interests do not override your fundamental rights and freedoms, particularly as we do not store personally identifiable information and only access aggregated analytics data.

Cookies

Our Website does not use cookies for analytics or tracking purposes.

Third-Party Services

Our Website uses the following third-party services:

  • Cloudflare: For hosting our analytics endpoint and content delivery. Cloudflare Privacy Policy
  • ipify: For retrieving visitor IP addresses for analytics purposes. ipify Website
  • Google Fonts: For typography. Google Privacy Policy
  • GitHub: Our source code and documentation are hosted on GitHub. GitHub Privacy Statement
  • jsDelivr CDN: For delivering the Pyodide Python runtime in our playground. jsDelivr Privacy Policy

Your Rights

Depending on your location, you may have certain rights regarding your personal data, including:

  • The right to access information we hold about you
  • The right to request correction of inaccurate data
  • The right to request deletion of your data
  • The right to object to processing
  • The right to data portability

As we do not store personally identifiable information, we are unable to identify or retrieve data specific to individual users.

Children's Privacy

Our Website is not directed at children under the age of 13, and we do not knowingly collect personal information from children.

Changes to This Policy

We may update this Privacy Policy from time to time. We will notify you of any changes by posting the new Privacy Policy on this page and updating the "Last updated" date.

In Shakespeare’s play Macbeth, madness is an important theme. Callousness towards murder and hysteria caused by it in the characters of Macbeth and Lady Macbeth, respectively, is an example of madness. Madness in Macbeth is depicted as an infectious disease that spreads among characters by supernatural forces, compulsion, guilt, and ambition. At the beginning of Shakespeare’s play Macbeth, the author uses the witches’ cryptic incantation, “Fair is foul, and foul is fair,” to set the stage for the play. This incantation introduces the audience to the madness that drives the characters of Macbeth and Lady Macbeth to their ultimate doom. Ambition is an important theme in Shakespeare’s play Macbeth that affects the characters in terms of madness and insanity. Shakespeare’s play Macbeth indicates that madness is a physiological and divine phenomenon. Shakespeare introduces the theme of madness from the beginning, but it is not as a psychological state of the individual, rather as a moral breakdown that is spread through the word. The witches blur the line of morality with their incantation of: "Fair is foul, and foul is fair: / Hover through the fog and filthy air" (Act 1, Scene 1). The antithesis of 'fair' and 'foul' blurs the line of morality between good and evil, and the fricative sounds are intended for the audience to hiss in unison with the witches in their descent into madness. For a Jacobean audience, who were familiar with King James I’s 'Daemonologie' (1597), in which witches are agents of the devil, the audience would have immediately understood the demonic threat from the beginning of the play. In relation to the structure of the play, Shakespeare has set the scene before the audience is introduced to the main character of Macbeth, and they are immersed in the moral ambiguity of the play from the beginning. Two scenes later, Macbeth recites the antithesis of the witches' incantation when he says: "So foul and fair a day I have not seen" (Act 1, Scene 3). This irony demonstrates the infiltration of Macbeth’s words by the words of the witches before he deliberately becomes involved in their prophecy, demonstrating the way in which madness can spread by the gradual corruption of language, as opposed to a sudden descent into madness. Through this piece, it can be seen that Macbeth’s aside demonstrates his increasing ambition, as his desire for the "stars [to] hide [their] fires" uses a metaphor to hide stars in the sky in order to prevent "light" from witnessing his "black and deep desires." The antithesis of "light" and the external world of "black" is the manifestation of his inner conflict, and it can be seen, through this aside, how Macbeth is hiding his madness from the external world. In the world of Jacobean England, in which King James I had narrowly escaped the Gunpowder Plot of 1605, this hidden regicide would have been a chilling part of the performance for the audience, demonstrating the way in which Shakespeare’s piece reinforced his perception of madness as festering in silence before bursting forth in violence. Both the Weird Sisters and Lady Macbeth are instrumental characters who use Macbeth’s ambition, leading to his eventual fall into psychological instability. Macbeth is welcomed by the Weird Sisters, who prophesise that he is the “Thane of Glamis,” the “Thane of Cawdor,” and the “King hereafter.” The hypnotic incantation by the Weird Sisters has enthralled Macbeth, who is introduced to the idea that leads him down the dark path to corruption. This scene can be analyzed in relation to King James’s *Daemonologie* published in 1597, in which he describes witches as instruments of corruption that threaten human souls. To the people in this period, the concept of witches is the reality of the supernatural world. Lady Macbeth uses ambition to become purpose by using Macbeth’s masculinity, which eventually leads to the act of regicide and the transgression of the moral codes set forth by the Great Chain of Being, a hierarchical structure in which God is at the top, followed by angels, the king, the nobility, the commoners, and then the animals, the plants, and the minerals. From the perspective of the play, the murder of King Duncan, God’s anointed king, is a transgression against the natural world and God’s will. The consequences of this transgression against the natural world and God’s will are illustrated through the drama that transpires after the murder of King Duncan. The natural world is thrown into chaos as the horses maul each other, the sun is eclipsed by darkness, and an owl kills a falcon. Macbeth’s psychological state is also reflective of the world’s disruption as a result of the murder, as he sees demons, suffers from insomnia, and experiences great guilt. The machinations of Lady Macbeth represent the Jacobean fear of women’s violation of the natural world. Her behavior and psychological manipulation of her husband would have been perceived as unnatural and threatening to the natural world. Therefore, it can be seen that the play by Shakespeare portrays the fall into madness by Macbeth as the result of the influence of the supernatural world and the machinations of women. The witches admit to their knowledge of the consequences of their actions when they state “By the pricking of my thumbs, / Something wicked this way comes” (Act 4, Scene 1), as Macbeth approaches. This reveals that he has become the very embodiment of evil that they had prophesied he would be. Macbeth's illusion that he is invincible is also nourished when he is told that he can be harmed by “None of woman born” (Act 4, Scene 1), instilling a delusional sense of invincibility, which leads to further deterioration of his state of mind. In the course of the play, Macbeth’s psychological change is depicted by his insensitivity to murder and his authoritarian leadership style. In the play, Macbeth confides in his “dear wife” his paranoia and obsession with his mind being “full of scorpions” as early as Act 3, Scene 2, because Fleance knows too much about the murder of King Duncan, causing his unstable mind to lead him to further violence and insensitivity to murder. Macbeth’s murder of King Duncan may also be seen in the context of the period’s fear of regicide in the aftermath of the Gunpowder Plot in 1605. This paranoia, however, can be seen in the audience’s perception because they would be doubtful that this could happen in real life. The guilt and fear that Macbeth showed can be seen by the audience in the period as a form of natural punishment for defying the Great Chain of Being and the Divine Right of Kings, the idea that monarchs derive their powers directly from God, and that the King is chosen by God, so his right to the throne cannot be questioned. This also meant that defying the King is not only treasonous but also a sin against God. Furthermore, Macbeth's megalomania is also displayed in the last acts of the play as a sign of his descent to madness. His impulsiveness and defiance are also displayed when he says, "I will not be afraid of death and bane, till Birnam Forest come to Dunsinane." (Act 5, Scene 3) His willingness to slaughter entire families, as displayed by his command to his men to "give to the edge o' the sword his wife, his babes," (Act 4, Scene 1) displays his cold-bloodedness as a man who has no further conscience or remorse for his actions. He does not think of or regret his actions, as displayed by his thoughts that "I have supped full with horrors" as cruelty becomes all too familiar to him. This would have been a powerful theme for a Jacobean audience who were plagued by fears of tyrannical leaders. The threat of political instability meant that a tyrant-king was a real fear for a nation that had seen many real-life leaders exhibit tyrannical behavior. As a leader who gained his position illegally, Macbeth is destined to become the tyrant-king that a nation fears most, thereby destroying himself and his people in the process. In addition to Macbeth's madness, Lady Macbeth's madness shows the ways in which guilt and trauma lead to psychological breakdown. Shakespeare first presents Lady Macbeth's deterioration in Scene 5, in which she is depicted as obsessively washing her hands because she believes she has blood on them. Her desperate question "Will these hands ne'er be clean?" shows her torment, which is in direct contrast to her previous ease in which she dismissed guilt in Act 2: “a little water will clear them of this dead” (Act 2, Scene 2). In addition, the doctor who is present in the scene asks, “When was it she last walked?”, which indicates that she is sleepwalking. To a Jacobean audience, this depiction of sleepwalking would have medical and spiritual connotations. Sleepwalking would have been a sign of daemonic possession, as King James I argued in his book Daemonologie. This is also in keeping with her previous depiction as being connected with the supernatural through her request for "unsex me here" (Act 1, Scene 5). Moreover, Lady Macbeth’s disjointed syntax is also an indicator of her madness. The haunting line "The Thane of Fife had a wife" (Act 5, Scene 1) reduces Lady Macbeth to a childlike demeanor through the use of a nursery rhyme. Furthermore, her confused speech would have been seen as a sign of hysteria, which is a kind of madness exclusive to women. The ways in which Lady Macbeth transgresses feminine roles through her aggression and ambition are now her ultimate downfall. A Jacobean audience would have seen this as divine retribution, an ultimate transgression against the Great Chain of Being and against the roles of women in the Renaissance. Essentially, madness is not only a theme in Macbeth, it is also a structural element of the play, ultimately leading to the tragic downfall of its titular character and his wife, as well as challenging morality by subverting the lines between right and wrong. The play is heavily influenced by King James I’s Daemonologie as well as other works of its time, such as the Great Chain of Being, in which a corrupted monarch, driven to madness by witchcraft and a manipulative wife, corrupts an entire kingdom. The play is of great use to a contemporary reader as a means of engaging with a Shakespearean tragedy as well as learning about the cultural and intellectual landscape of early seventeenth-century Europe.
