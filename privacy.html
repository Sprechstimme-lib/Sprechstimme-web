Privacy Policy

Last updated: December 15, 2025

This Privacy Policy describes how the Sprechstimme website ("we", "us", or "our") collects, uses, and handles information when you visit sprechstim.me (the "Website").

Information We Collect

We collect basic analytics data to understand how visitors interact with our Website. When you visit our Website, the following information is collected:

  • IP Address: Your IP address is retrieved via a third-party service (ipify) and sent to our analytics endpoint hosted on Cloudflare. We do not store your IP address; it is only used for aggregated analytics in the Cloudflare dashboard.
  • Page Views: Information about which pages you visit on our Website.
  • Timestamp: The date and time of your visit.

How We Use This Information

We use the analytics data to:

  • Understand how visitors use our Website
  • Improve our Website's content and user experience
  • Monitor the performance and availability of our Website

Data Storage

We do not store any personally identifiable information, including IP addresses, on our own servers. Analytics data is processed by Cloudflare and only aggregated, anonymized statistics are available to us through the Cloudflare dashboard.

Legal Basis for Processing (GDPR)

For visitors from the European Economic Area (EEA), United Kingdom, and Switzerland, we process this data under the legal basis of legitimate interests (Article 6(1)(f) of the GDPR). Our legitimate interests include:

  • Understanding website usage to improve our services
  • Ensuring the security and proper functioning of our Website
  • Analyzing trends to better serve our users

We have assessed that these interests do not override your fundamental rights and freedoms, particularly as we do not store personally identifiable information and only access aggregated analytics data.

Cookies

Our Website does not use cookies for analytics or tracking purposes.

Third-Party Services

Our Website uses the following third-party services:

  • Cloudflare: For hosting our analytics endpoint and content delivery. Cloudflare Privacy Policy
  • ipify: For retrieving visitor IP addresses for analytics purposes. ipify Website
  • Google Fonts: For typography. Google Privacy Policy
  • GitHub: Our source code and documentation are hosted on GitHub. GitHub Privacy Statement
  • jsDelivr CDN: For delivering the Pyodide Python runtime in our playground. jsDelivr Privacy Policy

Your Rights

Depending on your location, you may have certain rights regarding your personal data, including:

  • The right to access information we hold about you
  • The right to request correction of inaccurate data
  • The right to request deletion of your data
  • The right to object to processing
  • The right to data portability

As we do not store personally identifiable information, we are unable to identify or retrieve data specific to individual users.

Children's Privacy

Our Website is not directed at children under the age of 13, and we do not knowingly collect personal information from children.

Changes to This Policy

We may update this Privacy Policy from time to time. We will notify you of any changes by posting the new Privacy Policy on this page and updating the "Last updated" date.

Contact Us

If you have any questions about this Privacy Policy, please open an issue on our GitHub repository.

In Shakespeare's Macbeth, Madness is a central theme throughout the play. Using Macbeth’s and Lady Macbeth’s hysteria, madness is portrayed as a contagious disease that infects characters through supernatural methods. Shakespeare starts Macbeth with the witch’s chant “Fair is foul, and foul is fair”. This sets up the tone for the play and foreshadows the descent into hysteria which both protagonists will face. One cause of their madness is ambition, leading to many character’s insanity in the play. Overall, Shakespeare conveys that madness is conveyed through phsychological and divine means. From the beginning of the play, Shakespeare introduces madness as a moral breakdown rather than the character’s state of mind. The witch’s haunting chant "Fair is foul, and foul is fair: / Hover through the fog and filthy air” is a revealing example. The fricative of “foul” and “fair” (Act 1, Scene 1) blurs the line between good and evil for the reader and allows the audience to hiss in unison with the witches. For a Jacobean audience, who had read Daemonologie (1597), the threat would be apparent from the beginning of the play as witches are portrayed as agents of the devil. Furthermore, this extract appears before Macbeth actually appears, which creates ambiguity and suspense for the audience. Two scenes later, Macbeth is introduced with his famous line “So foul and fair a day I have not seen" (Act 1, Scene 3) mirroring the previous incantations of the witches and how they are to be involved in his prophecy. This demonstrates how madness can cause not just sudden change, but a slow degradation of language. As well as this, Macbeth demonstrates his increasing aspirations for power as he reveals his desire for “stars [to] hide [their] fires” (Act 1, Scene 4). This is used as a metaphor reflecting how he needs to prevent the “light” from witnessing his treasonous “desires”. The juxtaposition of the “light” and “black” shows torment and secrecy from the outside world. In the Shakespearean era, The Gunpowder Plot has recently occurred, so the regicide would have been alarming. This goes to show how Shakespeare’s piece reinforced his perception of madness as brewing in silence before bursting forth in violence. In Macbeth, the weird sisters and Lady Macbeth are portrayed as conspiring Macbeth’s descent into madness. At the beginning of the play, Macbeth is introduced to the weird sisters who prophesise him becoming “Thane of Glamis,” the “Thane of Cawdor,” and the “King hereafter.” (Act 1, Scene 3) This ritualistic incantation is arguably the start of Macbeth’s downfall into Madness. Using Daemonologie we can infer that the audience saw witches as having a real presence in the world. In King James’ book, their presentation as agents of the devil, closely mimics the action of the weird sisters in the play. Moreover, Lady Macbeth exploits Macbeth’s vulnerability by targeting his Masculinity. At the beginning of the play, by telling him that “When you durst do it, then you were a man; / And to be more than what you were, you would / Be so much more the man” (Act 1, Scene 7), she indirectly goes against the Great Chain of Being. This was an imagined hierarchical structure in which God was at the top, followed by angels, the king, the nobility, the commoners, the animals, the plants, and the minerals. By going against this chain, not only did Lady Macbeth commit regicide but sinned against god. The consequences of this can clearly be seen after King Duncan is killed. Shakespeare describes how an owl kills a falcon and the sun is eclipsed by darkness. This even extends to Lady Macbeth’s own sanity as she starts sleepwalking and incessantly rubbing a “spot” of invisible blood. Her behaviour and manipulation of her husband would have been seen as unnatural by the Jacobean audience at the time. The witches themselves see what they have caused as they mutter “By the pricking of my thumbs, / Something wicked this way comes” (Act 4, Scene 1) in their second encounter with Macbeth. Adding to their previous prophecies, they tell him that he can be killed by “none of woman born”, creating a delusional sense of security which ultimately leads to his downfall. As the play progresses, Macbeth is increasingly desensitised to his murders. He admits to his wife that his mind is “full of scorpions” (Act 3, Scene 2). This happens as Fleance, Banquo’s son, is increasingly certain that Macbeth is behind this regicide. This leads to Macbeth being even more desensitised to Murders through further mental breakdown. Due to the recent failed regicide at the time, the audience would have been paranoid as they would have doubted that this could happen in real life. The audience, furthermore, would have viewed Macbeth’s madness just a heavenly punishment for going against the Divine Right of Kings and the Great Chain of Being. In the Jacobean era, the Divine right of Kings was the belief that rulers attained their power through their affiliation to God. Macbeth’s new egotistical behaviour is seen as he enters the battle in the final acts of the play. With his newfound courage from the witches his defiance is portrayed through his words “I will not be afraid of death and bane, till Birnam Forest come to Dunsinane." (Act 5, Scene 3) His cold blooded behaviour arises as he orders his men to "give to the edge o' the sword his wife, his babes," (Act 4, Scene 1) His lack of regret or caring for his actions shown through the phrase “I have supped full with horrors” (Act 5, Scene 5) further shows his insensitivity to murders. In the audience at the time, this would carry much weight as there was an ever present fear of a tyrannical ruler as had occurred in the past. Macbeth here incarnates the population’s fears as he has gained power illegally, in the process bringing down his people and ultimately, himself. In Shakespeare’s Macbeth, Lady Macbeth’s madness shows how guilt from an indirect action can lead to a descent into madness. Through her guilt caused by the murder of King Duncan, she is casted by Shakespeare as obsessively washing her hands clean due to her belief of there being blood on them. Using the question: “Will these hands ne’er be clean” (Act 5, Scene 1), Shakespeare shows her affliction, which juxstaposes her previous calmness with which she handled herself in Act 2. She explains to Macbeth that “a little water will clear them of this deed” (Act 2, Scene 2). Her sleepwalking is portrayed by the doctor observing her, asking “when was it she last walked?” In the period where this play was performed, sleepwalking was generally believed to be a sign of demonic possession, hinting to Daemonologie, keeping in line with her previous depiction as being supernatural. This is seen as she asks to “unsex me here" (Act 1, Scene 5) Additionally, Lady Macbeth is reduced to a childlike demeanour through the use of a nursery rhyme: “The Thane of Fife had a wife” (Act 5, Scene 1) This is underpinned by her confused speech. To conclude, Lady Macbeth's fatal flaw was going against what women at the time should do.The audience back in the Jacobean era would perceive this as a direct retaliation from God.They committed sins against god and what women's social norms were during Shakespeare's time. Ultimately, in Shakespeare’s Macbeth, Madness is a key, central message which shapes the course of the character’s lives throught the play. Shakespeare drew much of his inspiration from Daemonologie as well as the recent news at the time, such as the Gunpowder plot. To the Jacobean audience, this would have been a reminder of the threat of tyrannical monarchs but to a modern day reader, this gives us insight into the culture of the Seventeenth century, in Europe.
