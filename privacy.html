Privacy Policy

Last updated: December 15, 2025

This Privacy Policy describes how the Sprechstimme website ("we", "us", or "our") collects, uses, and handles information when you visit sprechstim.me (the "Website").

Information We Collect

We collect basic analytics data to understand how visitors interact with our Website. When you visit our Website, the following information is collected:

  • IP Address: Your IP address is retrieved via a third-party service (ipify) and sent to our analytics endpoint hosted on Cloudflare. We do not store your IP address; it is only used for aggregated analytics in the Cloudflare dashboard.
  • Page Views: Information about which pages you visit on our Website.
  • Timestamp: The date and time of your visit.

How We Use This Information

We use the analytics data to:

  • Understand how visitors use our Website
  • Improve our Website's content and user experience
  • Monitor the performance and availability of our Website

Data Storage

We do not store any personally identifiable information, including IP addresses, on our own servers. Analytics data is processed by Cloudflare and only aggregated, anonymized statistics are available to us through the Cloudflare dashboard.

Legal Basis for Processing (GDPR)

For visitors from the European Economic Area (EEA), United Kingdom, and Switzerland, we process this data under the legal basis of legitimate interests (Article 6(1)(f) of the GDPR). Our legitimate interests include:

  • Understanding website usage to improve our services
  • Ensuring the security and proper functioning of our Website
  • Analyzing trends to better serve our users

We have assessed that these interests do not override your fundamental rights and freedoms, particularly as we do not store personally identifiable information and only access aggregated analytics data.

Cookies

Our Website does not use cookies for analytics or tracking purposes.

Third-Party Services

Our Website uses the following third-party services:

  • Cloudflare: For hosting our analytics endpoint and content delivery. Cloudflare Privacy Policy
  • ipify: For retrieving visitor IP addresses for analytics purposes. ipify Website
  • Google Fonts: For typography. Google Privacy Policy
  • GitHub: Our source code and documentation are hosted on GitHub. GitHub Privacy Statement
  • jsDelivr CDN: For delivering the Pyodide Python runtime in our playground. jsDelivr Privacy Policy

Your Rights

Depending on your location, you may have certain rights regarding your personal data, including:

  • The right to access information we hold about you
  • The right to request correction of inaccurate data
  • The right to request deletion of your data
  • The right to object to processing
  • The right to data portability

As we do not store personally identifiable information, we are unable to identify or retrieve data specific to individual users.

Children's Privacy

Our Website is not directed at children under the age of 13, and we do not knowingly collect personal information from children.

Changes to This Policy

We may update this Privacy Policy from time to time. We will notify you of any changes by posting the new Privacy Policy on this page and updating the "Last updated" date.

Contact Us

If you have any questions about this Privacy Policy, please open an issue on our GitHub repository.

Arthur Miller’s play A View from the Bridge is set in a tight-knit Italian-American neighbourhood in an immigrant community in New York (Red Hook, Brooklyn). Writing during a period of intense suspicion towards immigrants and outsiders in McCarthy-era America, Arthur Miller uses the theme of domestic tragedy to explore desire, betrayal, and the transition from innocence to experience. In this world, Miller introduces Eddie Carbone and his surrogate daughter, Catherine, who is standing at the threshold between childhood and adulthood. Miller begins A View From The Bridge in media res, with Catherine presented as being on the brink of significant change. After Eddie, a “husky figure” who does gruelling work as a longshoreman, returns home, Catherine, “running her hands over her skirt,” asks Eddie if he “likes[s] it.” This need for approval shows that, at the core, Catherine is still a child, but has a desire to grow and experiment with new styles as she has also “done her hair differently”. This question, however, gives Eddie power as he quickly affirms, “I think it’s too short, ain’t it”? Here, the question tag is an affirmative response that Catherine’s clothing is inappropriate. Through this initial interaction, Miller introduces Catherine as a character who yearns for new freedoms but is not yet bold enough to challenge the patriarchal authority figure in her life directly. Arguably, Catherine is not yet bold enough to challenge his opinions, leading her to be in “tears because he disapproves”, another sign of her still childish character. However, as the scene continues to unfold, she reveals to Eddie that she could make “50 dollars a week” by going to work as a typist. Her desire to take up the work is symbolic in the sense that by earning “50 dollars a week,” Catherine would have gained her independence. Moreover, her going to work in an office is significant in the sense that by doing so, she would have left the Carbone apartment, which is not only the main setting of the play but is also a symbol of the sphere of authority of Eddie. By stepping out of the apartment, Catherine would have literally distanced herself from the sphere of authority of Eddie. Perhaps the most pivotal point of transformation for Catherine, where she undergoes a change, is the scene where Catherine has sex with Rodolpho. Catherine’s change stems from the fact that she is prompted to do so by Rodolpho’s metaphorical description of Catherine as a "little bird" who has never been permitted to use her wings. Catherine is forced to confront the true nature of Eddie through Rodolpho’s metaphorical statement. Catherine realises that throughout her life, she has been systematically denied all her freedoms by Eddie, who has confined her to the confines of the domestic space of the Carbone apartment. Significantly, Catherine begins the scene by stating, "I’m afraid of Eddie here." In this statement, the adjective "afraid" is significant as it is one of the few times throughout the play where Catherine expresses her true feelings regarding the nature of Eddie. Moreover, the adverb "here" has an important double meaning. It not only represents the apartment where Catherine had been held captive by Eddie throughout the play, but also represents Catherine’s mind, which had also been held captive by Eddie. With this realisation, Catherine begs Rodolpho to "teach [her]". With the imperative verb, Catherine is exercising the free will that she has come to possess. Catherine’s choice to go to Rodolpho instead of Eddie, who had played the role of a father to her throughout the play, represents a choice that Catherine makes to go from the patriarchal figure who had tried to control her to the figure who had shown her the possibility for freedom. Catherine’s choice to become sexually involved with Rodolpho represents a choice that goes against the idea of the "Madonna type" that had been forced on her by Eddie throughout the play. Miller’s choice to represent Catherine’s sexual development represents a pivotal moment for the character as she challenges the patriarchal figures who had tried to control her throughout the play. Miller’s choice to represent Catherine’s sexual development as a private act rather than a public act represents the fact that Catherine’s development as a character represents the motivation behind the public act that Catherine chooses to commit in the final act of the play. Miller describes Eddie's betrayal of Rodolpho at that moment of the play when Catherine's relationship with Eddie changes to one characterised by hostility and bitterness. Miller leads up to that climax with a series of scenes that depict Catherine becoming more mature and asserting her independence. Catherine makes her first move towards independence when she decides to leave the Carbone apartment with Rodolpho, which is a stark contrast to the girl who was once reduced to “tears” because of his disapproval of her skirt. Similarly, her language when she tells Eddie that she is ‘going to get married, Eddie’ is no longer tentative or seeking permission. However, she only completes her transformation when Eddie calls immigration on Rodolpho and Marco. Once Marco exposes Eddie’s betrayal to the whole neighbourhood, Catherine unleashes a fury and self-confidence which would have been unthinkable at the start of the play, calling her uncle a “rat” who “belongs in the sewers”. The zoomorphism depicting Eddie as a repulsive creature strips him of his patriarchal dignity. Similarly, she exclaims that he “bites people in their sleep”, which is an act of cowardly betrayal. Moreover, this act of betrayal by Eddie is a violation of the cultural code that is deeply ingrained in the Italian-American community, which is the Omertà code that Miller has established as being sacred, and it is Catherine who points out this violation. Miller is showing that Catherine has not only grown up and attained her independence from Eddie but has also attained true integrity. In the course of the play, Miller follows Catherine through the difficult transformation from a dependent child to an independent adult. Significantly, however, Miller demonstrates the true tragedy not to be Catherine's new adulthood but Eddie's failure to cope with it. Miller thereby uses the play to examine the destructive power of possessive love and the conflict between individual freedom and patriarchal domination. In the final confrontation between Catherine and Eddie, which ends with Marco killing Eddie, Miller leaves the audience with a devastating finale. By having Eddie die a tragic death at the end of the play, Miller suggests that Catherine's emancipation is as much a moment of grief as it is of liberation.
