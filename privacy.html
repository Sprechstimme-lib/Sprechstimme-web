Privacy Policy

Last updated: December 15, 2025

This Privacy Policy describes how the Sprechstimme website ("we", "us", or "our") collects, uses, and handles information when you visit sprechstim.me (the "Website").

Information We Collect

We collect basic analytics data to understand how visitors interact with our Website. When you visit our Website, the following information is collected:

  • IP Address: Your IP address is retrieved via a third-party service (ipify) and sent to our analytics endpoint hosted on Cloudflare. We do not store your IP address; it is only used for aggregated analytics in the Cloudflare dashboard.
  • Page Views: Information about which pages you visit on our Website.
  • Timestamp: The date and time of your visit.

How We Use This Information

We use the analytics data to:

  • Understand how visitors use our Website
  • Improve our Website's content and user experience
  • Monitor the performance and availability of our Website

Data Storage

We do not store any personally identifiable information, including IP addresses, on our own servers. Analytics data is processed by Cloudflare and only aggregated, anonymized statistics are available to us through the Cloudflare dashboard.

Legal Basis for Processing (GDPR)

For visitors from the European Economic Area (EEA), United Kingdom, and Switzerland, we process this data under the legal basis of legitimate interests (Article 6(1)(f) of the GDPR). Our legitimate interests include:

  • Understanding website usage to improve our services
  • Ensuring the security and proper functioning of our Website
  • Analyzing trends to better serve our users

We have assessed that these interests do not override your fundamental rights and freedoms, particularly as we do not store personally identifiable information and only access aggregated analytics data.

Cookies

Our Website does not use cookies for analytics or tracking purposes.

Third-Party Services

Our Website uses the following third-party services:

  • Cloudflare: For hosting our analytics endpoint and content delivery. Cloudflare Privacy Policy
  • ipify: For retrieving visitor IP addresses for analytics purposes. ipify Website
  • Google Fonts: For typography. Google Privacy Policy
  • GitHub: Our source code and documentation are hosted on GitHub. GitHub Privacy Statement
  • jsDelivr CDN: For delivering the Pyodide Python runtime in our playground. jsDelivr Privacy Policy

Your Rights

Depending on your location, you may have certain rights regarding your personal data, including:

  • The right to access information we hold about you
  • The right to request correction of inaccurate data
  • The right to request deletion of your data
  • The right to object to processing
  • The right to data portability

As we do not store personally identifiable information, we are unable to identify or retrieve data specific to individual users.

Children's Privacy

Our Website is not directed at children under the age of 13, and we do not knowingly collect personal information from children.

Changes to This Policy

We may update this Privacy Policy from time to time. We will notify you of any changes by posting the new Privacy Policy on this page and updating the "Last updated" date.

Contact Us

In Owen’s ‘Disabled’, Frost’s ‘Out, Out-’, and Chopin’s ‘The Story of an Hour’, isolation and loneliness are central topics which shape the experience of the protagonists. While Frost’s remote setting conveys a sense of seclusion, Owen’s poem shows the devastating loneliness of a war veteran. On the other hand, Chopin’s short story shows that loneliness can also be a positive state. Overall, these texts show the stark differences between the concepts of loneliness. Owen’s poem ‘Disabled’ juxtaposes memories and present-day experiences to emphasise the soldier's feeling of loneliness. The structure of the poem alternates between descriptions of the soldier’s past vitality and his present-day desolation, with phrases such as 'About this time' and 'In the old times' alerting the reader to the juxtaposition of these two realities. This becomes clear at the beginning of the second stanza, where the soldier reminisces about the fact that ‘Town used to swing so gay / When glow lamps budded in the light-blue trees’. The liveliness of the scenery that is evoked through the verb ‘budded’ contrasts with the bleak image of the soldier sitting in a ‘wheeled chair, waiting for dark’ that opens the poem. The verb 'budded' here is particularly evocative, drawing on natural imagery of spring growth to suggest renewal, a stark contrast to the soldier's now monotonous existence, confined to his 'wheeled chair'. In his memory, 'girls glanced lovelier as the air grew dim'; now, 'All of them touch him like some queer disease.' Through the simile 'like some queer disease', Owen highlights how the soldier is perceived like a contagious body by others, thus further emphasising his isolation from society. Finally, this also becomes apparent in the poem's structure as nostalgic memories become increasingly supplanted by bleak prospects of the future. In the final stanza, Owen suggests that loneliness is an inescapable and permanent consequence of experiencing war trauma. The stanza breaks from the alternating structure of the rest of the poem, shifting away from its present- and future-tense pattern, as if the memory has finally been exhausted. The phrase 'a few sick years in institutes' foreshadows both the soldier's ever more isolated existence but may also imply that he only has a few years left to live.The noun 'institutes' is intentionally unsympathetic, stripping the soldier of any warmth or humanity and condemning his remaining years to a series of cold, bureaucratic places. Owen also uses the auxiliary verb 'will' repeatedly in this stanza, which locks the soldier into a passive, predetermined future from which he cannot escape. The continuation in 'the women’s eyes / Passed from him to the strong men that were whole' most clearly shows this, with the line break mirroring the soldier’s passive and invisible role. Finally, the poem highlights the soldier's permanent state of isolation through the rhetorical question in the last line. The soldier's plea 'Why don't they hear me?' undercuts any sense of closure and instead presents the soldier in a state of indefinite despair and isolation. Throughout 'Out, Out-', Frost uses visual imagery to create a feeling of isolation. The initial depiction of the saw as a force that 'leaped out of the boy’s hand' immediately establishes the boy as powerless in the environment in which the event occurs. Frost’s interjection 'call it a day I wish they had said' is also significant because the use of the subjunctive 'might have' indicates that no one did intervene on the boy’s behalf, emphasising the bureaucratic adult world and the boy’s isolation. Furthermore, the emotional phrase 'doing a man's work, though a child at heart' establishes the disparity between the roles that the world has placed upon him and the innocence he feels. The word 'though' comprises the tragedy that makes up the poem. Frost’s poem was inspired by an actual event which took place in 1910 in Bethlehem, New Hampshire, and the rural New England setting in which the event transpires normalises the use of child labor. This makes the callous views of the townspeople not only uncaring but also culturally ingrained, emphasising the isolation of the boy in the society in which he transpires. In the second half of the poem, the presentation of the subdued reactions to the boy’s fate emphasise his isolation. The boy's desperate appeal 'Don't let him cut my hand off' uses the third person 'him' to refer to the doctor as if he were some kind of distant and menacing figure rather than someone there to help him. Frost’s deliberate use of observation with 'the watcher at his pulse took fright' reduces the boy to a mere medical condition, with the word 'watcher' implying observation rather than compassion or concern for the boy's fate. The heart-wrenching finality of 'No more to build on there' has a double entendre: literally, there are no further heartbeats to fuel life; but Frost also implies that the community has no further use for grief. The final line 'they turned to their affairs' uses the plural noun 'they' to encompass everyone's reactions; it reduces every individual to collective blame and responsibility for the boy's fate. The word 'turn' implies a physical and emotional turning away from the boy; while the word 'affairs' reduces all human life to business. The isolation of the boy is complete and eternal. Chopin’s 'The Story of an Hour' depicts loneliness as a positive source for self-discovery. When the female protagonist Mrs. Mallard hears of her husband’s death, the statement 'she would have no one follow her' does not simply indicate her grief over losing her husband; it is an assertion of her need for solitude, emphasised by the modal verb 'would', which indicates quiet determination. Chopin slows down the pace of her story as Mrs. Mallard enters her bedroom, 'pressed down by physical exertion that haunted her body and seemed to reach into her soul'. The personification of 'haunted' gives the impression that her turmoil does not result from her husband’s death but from years of her repression of her selfhood. Chopin’s portrayal of Mrs. Mallard’s stare as a 'suspension of intelligent thought' is structurally significant as it prepares the reader for her discovery at the end of the story. Mrs. Mallard whispered 'free, free, free!' highlights that loneliness is associated with freedom and emancipation in Chopin’s story. Throughout the story, Chopin uses natural imagery to present isolation as positive and liberating. After hearing the news, Mrs. Mallard moves towards the open window where 'the delicious breath of rain was in the air.' The anthropomorphism of nature through the 'breath' of rain conveys a sense of closeness and the adjective 'delicious' shifts the sense of isolation from one of sorrow to one of nourishment. Furthermore, Mrs. Mallard’s awakening to freedom is itself provided through the natural world, as the realisation of freedom 'creeping out of the sky, reaching toward her through the sounds, the scents, the color that filled the air'. This tricolon implies that freedom and self-assertion are provided by nature, not by marriage. The contrast is striking, as Mrs. Mallard feels more connected with the natural world, through the sparrows and raindrops, than any other human being in the house. However, the oxymoron of the final line ('the joy that kills'), makes it clear that this freedom is only available through isolation. When it is revealed at the end of the story that her husband is not dead after all, Mrs Mallard’s freedom is extinguished. In conclusion, the ideas about loneliness differ in each of the three texts. In Owen’s 'Disabled', loneliness is presented as a devastating force that leaves the war veteran trapped in grief. In Frost’s 'Out, Out-', isolation is portrayed through the indifferent reactions to a tragic death. On the other hand, Chopin’s story reframes loneliness and isolation as liberating. Together, these texts show how isolation can be interpreted as alienating, destroying or liberating.
