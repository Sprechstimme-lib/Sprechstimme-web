Privacy Policy

Last updated: December 15, 2025

This Privacy Policy describes how the Sprechstimme website ("we", "us", or "our") collects, uses, and handles information when you visit sprechstim.me (the "Website").

Information We Collect

We collect basic analytics data to understand how visitors interact with our Website. When you visit our Website, the following information is collected:

  • IP Address: Your IP address is retrieved via a third-party service (ipify) and sent to our analytics endpoint hosted on Cloudflare. We do not store your IP address; it is only used for aggregated analytics in the Cloudflare dashboard.
  • Page Views: Information about which pages you visit on our Website.
  • Timestamp: The date and time of your visit.

How We Use This Information

We use the analytics data to:

  • Understand how visitors use our Website
  • Improve our Website's content and user experience
  • Monitor the performance and availability of our Website

Data Storage

We do not store any personally identifiable information, including IP addresses, on our own servers. Analytics data is processed by Cloudflare and only aggregated, anonymized statistics are available to us through the Cloudflare dashboard.

Legal Basis for Processing (GDPR)

For visitors from the European Economic Area (EEA), United Kingdom, and Switzerland, we process this data under the legal basis of legitimate interests (Article 6(1)(f) of the GDPR). Our legitimate interests include:

  • Understanding website usage to improve our services
  • Ensuring the security and proper functioning of our Website
  • Analyzing trends to better serve our users

We have assessed that these interests do not override your fundamental rights and freedoms, particularly as we do not store personally identifiable information and only access aggregated analytics data.

Cookies

Our Website does not use cookies for analytics or tracking purposes.

Third-Party Services

Our Website uses the following third-party services:

  • Cloudflare: For hosting our analytics endpoint and content delivery. Cloudflare Privacy Policy
  • ipify: For retrieving visitor IP addresses for analytics purposes. ipify Website
  • Google Fonts: For typography. Google Privacy Policy
  • GitHub: Our source code and documentation are hosted on GitHub. GitHub Privacy Statement
  • jsDelivr CDN: For delivering the Pyodide Python runtime in our playground. jsDelivr Privacy Policy

Your Rights

Depending on your location, you may have certain rights regarding your personal data, including:

  • The right to access information we hold about you
  • The right to request correction of inaccurate data
  • The right to request deletion of your data
  • The right to object to processing
  • The right to data portability

As we do not store personally identifiable information, we are unable to identify or retrieve data specific to individual users.

Children's Privacy

Our Website is not directed at children under the age of 13, and we do not knowingly collect personal information from children.

Changes to This Policy

We may update this Privacy Policy from time to time. We will notify you of any changes by posting the new Privacy Policy on this page and updating the "Last updated" date.

Contact Us

If you have any questions about this Privacy Policy, please open an issue on our GitHub repository.

A spectral blue shadow cast a ghostly glow upon the whitewashed walls. From his vantage point, he gazed upon the city beneath: lights were shimmering along the ribbon of water snaking between jagged glass shards clawing at the sky. The towering panes of hued glass, stretching from floor to ceiling, stood as a barrier between him and the abyss below. He reached out, hands pale. He could feel the glass now, cold to the touch, and his breath creating a haze of lights in front of him. It was time. There was a knock. He looked at the doors, head held high, and approached his throne flanking a mahogany table. Ensconced in his chair, he beckoned the visitor in. A slender figure entered, his footsteps dampened by the thick carmine carpets; in his left hand, he held up a black leather bag. He sat, swallowed by the blinding darkness. “Good morning.” The monotone voice echoed from the end of the table. The other man flinched; he was trying to keep a serious face. “I think we’re missing someone?” A deafening silence. Kyle did not dare speak - he knew what had happened to the others. He saw their skeletons suspended on thin garroting wires from the ceiling. The names of victims were engraved into the bones. Only one spot was left: a spot for Macbeth. The phone rang, breaking the tension in the room. The boss picked it up. “Paddock calls”, he said, a disgruntled expression on his pale face. After a moment of intense concentration with a series of nods, he finally put down the receiver. Unable to tell what had happened, Kyle surreptitiously followed him into the lift. He felt claustrophobic; it was the last place he wanted to be. The doors shut, and they started their journey into the inky shaft. A few hundred meters below, Paddock pushed through the heavy double doors of the reception which closed behind him, sealing him in. His clothes were heavy from the torrent of water gushing down from the dark heavens. He looked around. Panelled walls, marble floors, chandeliers. The thought of his paycheck rekindled his resentment. He took a deep breath: his hands trembled and suddenly the chime of the lift doors opened Pandora’s box and out stepped the two men to confront Paddock. They looked into the depths of his eyes and motioned towards a seat. It was unlike any object in the lavishly adorned room, only bare metal, its raw flesh exposed to the world. Paddock had no choice. He sat trembling. “Cold?” the boss asked. Paddock looked up seeing a draconian smile plastered on his face. He nodded slowly. “Good! Now I believe you had a job to carry out?” Another slow nod. “So the deed is done?” Another slow nod. “And no one knows?” Yet another nod. The boss paced around approvingly and looked at Kyle whose eyes were drifting towards the large billboard outside. He in turn looked to see Macbeth smiling back at him, his eyes boring into him, as though he wanted a vista into his soul. He forced himself to look away only to see the billboard shouting at him: “Macbeth for president!” His expression changed and a smile crept back onto his face. Macbeth was dead, nobody could stop him. The billboard flickered and scrolled to the next page. He saw his reflection on the screen staring back and, for a moment, he thought he saw blood dripping down from the side of the digital smile - but it was only rain. Only rain. Or so he told himself. “Sir.” He snapped out of his trance and swivelled to look at Paddock who had stood up to meet his gaze. He had an outstretched arm reaching out expectantly, palm up - waiting not for a gift but a reckoning. “My money. Now.” He had a grave, monotone voice. The boss was taken by surprise. How dare he speak to him like this? He was going to be the next president! Kyle looked at him expectantly, and as he looked back he was met with chilly blue eyes. He had no choice. It was a fight he could not win, so he handed over the briefcase to Paddock’s beckoning hand. Paddock didn’t smile. He didn’t nod and took a chocolate out of his pocket. Without looking at the other men, he opened his mouth wide to swallow it. Only now could the boss see his mouth. The yellow teeth and that tongue. A tongue like a serpent looking for prey. It constricted around the brown cube in his hands and brought it back into its lair as the doors shut again. “Good day, sir”, Paddock said with forceful amicability. Kyle directed him towards the doors. A sudden gust of icy air swept in, followed by silence until warmth reclaimed dominion. At the reception desk, the phone rang. Both men looked at it in awe. So diminutive yet commanding immense authority — the small device had the power to grab the attention of an entire room. He stepped towards it and picked up the shaking object. The two were trembling in unison, he put it next to his pale ear. “Brian!” the boss flinched. He was not used to being called by his name. But that voice he did recognise, it was Paddock. “When shall we three meet again?” He looked at Kyle but to no avail, he was on his phone. “When the election is won.” “Where is the place?” “I have yet to decide.”
