Privacy Policy

Last updated: December 15, 2025

This Privacy Policy describes how the Sprechstimme website ("we", "us", or "our") collects, uses, and handles information when you visit sprechstim.me (the "Website").

Information We Collect

We collect basic analytics data to understand how visitors interact with our Website. When you visit our Website, the following information is collected:

  • IP Address: Your IP address is retrieved via a third-party service (ipify) and sent to our analytics endpoint hosted on Cloudflare. We do not store your IP address; it is only used for aggregated analytics in the Cloudflare dashboard.
  • Page Views: Information about which pages you visit on our Website.
  • Timestamp: The date and time of your visit.

How We Use This Information

We use the analytics data to:

  • Understand how visitors use our Website
  • Improve our Website's content and user experience
  • Monitor the performance and availability of our Website

Data Storage

We do not store any personally identifiable information, including IP addresses, on our own servers. Analytics data is processed by Cloudflare and only aggregated, anonymized statistics are available to us through the Cloudflare dashboard.

Legal Basis for Processing (GDPR)

For visitors from the European Economic Area (EEA), United Kingdom, and Switzerland, we process this data under the legal basis of legitimate interests (Article 6(1)(f) of the GDPR). Our legitimate interests include:

  • Understanding website usage to improve our services
  • Ensuring the security and proper functioning of our Website
  • Analyzing trends to better serve our users

We have assessed that these interests do not override your fundamental rights and freedoms, particularly as we do not store personally identifiable information and only access aggregated analytics data.

Cookies

Our Website does not use cookies for analytics or tracking purposes.

Third-Party Services

Our Website uses the following third-party services:

  • Cloudflare: For hosting our analytics endpoint and content delivery. Cloudflare Privacy Policy
  • ipify: For retrieving visitor IP addresses for analytics purposes. ipify Website
  • Google Fonts: For typography. Google Privacy Policy
  • GitHub: Our source code and documentation are hosted on GitHub. GitHub Privacy Statement
  • jsDelivr CDN: For delivering the Pyodide Python runtime in our playground. jsDelivr Privacy Policy

Your Rights

Depending on your location, you may have certain rights regarding your personal data, including:

  • The right to access information we hold about you
  • The right to request correction of inaccurate data
  • The right to request deletion of your data
  • The right to object to processing
  • The right to data portability

As we do not store personally identifiable information, we are unable to identify or retrieve data specific to individual users.

Children's Privacy

Our Website is not directed at children under the age of 13, and we do not knowingly collect personal information from children.

Changes to This Policy

We may update this Privacy Policy from time to time. We will notify you of any changes by posting the new Privacy Policy on this page and updating the "Last updated" date.

Contact Us

If you have any questions about this Privacy Policy, please open an issue on our GitHub repository.

In Owen’s ‘Disabled’, Frost’s ‘Out, Out-’, and Chopin’s ‘The Story of an Hour’, isolation and loneliness are central topics which shape the experience of the protagonists. Frost’s remote setting conveys a sense of isolation and Owen’s poem shows the devastating loneliness of a war veteran. On the other hand, Chopin’s short story shows that loneliness can also be a positive state. Overall, these texts show the large angles of the concepts of loneliness. In Owen’s poem disabled, the poet uses juxtaposition to create a feeling of isolation. In the poem, the structure alternates between the soldier’s energy in the past and his lonliness in the present. The time phrases: ‘About this time' and 'In the old times' makes this come across. In the second stanza, this juxtaposition is strengthened by the fact that ‘Town used to swing so gay / When glow lamps budded in the light-blue trees’, with the energy conveyed through the word ‘budded’. This has the connotation of rejuvenation and new life while the contrasting phrase: ‘wheeled chair, waiting for dark’ which exposes his monotonous life in a ‘wheeled chair’. This contrast is further put through by his memory of 'girls glanced lovelier as the air grew dim' and now 'All of them touch him like some queer disease.' His isolation is oconveyed through the simile: ‘like some queer disease’, which further distances him from society. Finally, this also becomes apparent in the poem's structure as nostalgic memories become increasingly supplanted by bleak prospects of the future. In the second stanza, the atmosphere changes with Owen suggesting that loneliness is an inescapable and permanent consequence of experiencing war trauma. Divulging from the alternate sequence present in the latter half of the poem, it mimics the exhaustion of the soldier’s memory. This is due to the ceasing of shift from past to future tenses. ‘A few sick years in institutes,’ foreshadows that he only has a few years left to live while reinforcing the isolation he experiences. By using the noun ‘instututes’, Owen creates a hostile, bureaucratic environment and strips the soldier of humanity in tandem. Owen also uses the auxilary verb ‘will’. This helps lock the soldier in the poem into a predetermined future from which he cannot escape. The use of 'the women’s eyes / Passed from him to the strong men that were whole' illustrates this clearly. The line break encapsulates the soldier's passive and passive role within the scene. Ultimately, through the rhetorical question in the final line of the poem, the reader is faced with an uncomfortable truth. The soldier is forevermore isolated from society. Asking 'Why don't they hear me?' dismisses any resolution and replaces it with one of indefinite hopelessness and solitude. In ‘Out-out’, Frost uses visual imagery to create a feeling of isolation. The saw is initially presented as wielding power as it ‘leaped out of the boy’s hand’ foreshadowing the sequence of events which unfold. By using the phrase 'call it a day I wish they had said' is also significant. The use of the subjunctive 'might have' shows how the bureaucratic adult world isolates the boy further. Moreover, the phrase 'doing a man's work, though a child at heart' incites emotion in the reader. It shows them how innocent he is. The word ‘though’ is especially emportant in this poem as Frost drew inspiration from actual events for his text. In Bethlehem, New Hampshire (1910), a young Boy’s hand was cut by a buzz saw, showing how child labour can leadd to tragedies. Additionally, the views of the townspeople become not only uncaring but also culturally ingrained. This emphasises the isolation of the boy in society. In the second half of the poem, the presentation of the subdued reactions to the boy’s fate emphasise his isolation. Through his final efforts to not 'let him cut my hand off', he uses the third person. This portrays the doctor as a menacing figure rather than someone trying to save him. Furthermore, Frost uses an observation of 'the watcher at his pulse took fright' further objectfies the boy. The noun ‘watcher’ strips the boy from recieving any compassion, reducing him to a medical condition rather than a human which is amplified by the surprisingly monotonous ‘No more to build on there’. This double entendre implies that there are no more heartbeats to fuel his life and that the townspeople have no need for grief. Finally, the reactions of the people are reflected through the line 'they turned to their affairs', with the plural noun ‘they’ helping encompass the whole population and distancing them from the boy. It also suggests that everyone is responsible for his death. The word ‘affairs’ reduces the human’s life to buisiness, reinforcing the boy’s loneliness in death. In Chopin’s 'The Story of an Hour' loneliness is portrayed as a path of self discovery. Mrs. Malard (the protagonist of the short story) hears about her husband's death, and her intentional isolation is indicated by the phrase: ‘she would have no one follow her’. The imperative ‘would’ shows her determination. In her text, Chopin then describes Mrs. Mallard as ‘pressed down by physical exertion that haunted her body and seemed to reach into her soul’. This also conveys how her turmoil does not come from her husband's death but rather from years of repression. This is shown through the personification of ‘haunted’ in the passage. Chopin describes Mrs. Mallard’s stare as a ‘suspension of intelligent thought’. This is structurally significant because it forshadows her discovery which occurs at the end of the passage: that her husband is indeed not dead. The repetition of ‘free, free, free!’ links isolation with hapiness in the text. Chopin uses natural imagery throughout the passage to portray isolation as liberating. Once Mrs. Mallard hears the news of her husband’s death, she inhales the ‘the delicious breath of rain [...] in the air.’ Through the use of the word ‘breath’, Chopin conveys a sense of closeness for the reader. The adjective ‘delicious’ also helps shift the isolation from sorrow to newfound energy. Chopin sustains this impression by reflecting the freedom in the natural world. Her freedom is realised by the 'creeping out of the sky, reaching toward her through the sounds, the scents, the color that filled the air', with the tricolon implying that freedom is provided by nature, not by marriage. Through the natural imagery, the author explains to the reader that it is not human beings which Mrs. Mallard feels connected to, instead it is nature such as the sparrows and raindrops. At the end of the text, it is revealed to Mrs. Mallard that her husband is not dead after all. Finally, the oxymoron of the ‘joy that kills’ shows that freedom is only achieved through isolation. In conclusion, in Owen's ‘Disabled’, Frost’s ‘Out, Out-’, and Chopin’s ‘The Story of an Hour’, the concept of lineliness differs. On one hand, In ‘Disabled’ lonliness is portrayed for the war veteran as resulting in grief. This is also the case with ‘Out, Out-’ where isolation is portrayed through the various actions of the townspeople whereas on the other hand, ‘The story of an hour’ shows how freedom can only be attained with isolation. These texts show how isolation can be perceived in different ways: alienating, destruction or even liberation.
